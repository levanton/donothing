# Onboarding story copy — variants

Working file for the three narrative screens (nostalgia → rushing → phoneSymptom).
The applied set lives at the top; everything below is archive and options.

## ✅ APPLIED — "Combo 1: the time thread" (2026-06-10)

The arc reads as one story about time: it used to stop → now it blurs away →
what if you stopped it again. "nowhere to be" mirrors "one more task";
"brings you back" answers "remember?".

### Screen 1 — "remember?" (NostalgiaScreen)

```
remember?

whole afternoons in the grass.
clouds drifting by.
nowhere to be.

time just… stopped.
```

### Screen 2 — "now." (RushingScreen)

```
now.

one more task.
one more message.
one more day gone.

it keeps us busy.
but never really here.

days, months, years — gone in a blur.
```

### Screen 3 — "what if…" (PhoneSymptomScreen)

```
what if…

you could stop time again?

one minute.
no phone. no plans.
just you.

even one minute a day
brings you back.
```

## Archive — replaced texts (in code until 2026-06-10)

### Screen 1 — "remember?"

```
remember?

lying in the grass.
staring at clouds.
dreaming about nothing.

time just… stopped.
```

(An earlier version ended with "no rush. / no goals. / no phone." — cut
because it mirrored the "now." screen's message too directly.)

### Screen 2 — "now."

```
now.

so much to do.
so many things calling.
so much pressure.

it keeps us busy.
but never really here.

days, months, years — gone in a blur.
```

Problem: the first three lines were abstract near-synonyms — no escalation.

### Screen 3 — "what if…"

```
what if…

you just stop.
for one minute.

put the world on pause.
and just be.

even one minute a day
can change your life.
```

Problem: "put the world on pause / and just be" was mushy filler;
"can change your life" read as infomercial cliché.

## Rejected alternatives

### Screen 1

**B — second-person immersion** ("clouds going nowhere" nods to Pico Iyer):

```
the grass under your back.
clouds going nowhere.
a whole day in your hands.

time just… stopped.
```

**C — brand-word payoff** (keeps old trio, swaps payoff):

```
lying in the grass.
staring at clouds.
dreaming about nothing.

and nothing was enough.
```

### Screen 2

**B — staccato verbs** (best fit for the line-drop animation, but loses
"it keeps us busy"):

```
wake. rush. work.
reply. plan. fix.
repeat.

always doing the next thing.
never really here.

days, months, years — gone in a blur.
```

**C — "always" with distinct objects** (place → task → person):

```
always somewhere to be.
always something to finish.
always someone waiting.

life became a to-do list.
and you're never really here.

days, months, years — gone in a blur.
```

**D — Lennon-style inversion** (shortest, hardest):

```
so much to do.
so little to feel.

busy planning life.
too busy to live it.

days, months, years — gone in a blur.
```

### Screen 3

**B — Anne Lamott structure** ("Almost everything will work again if you
unplug it for a few minutes, including you"):

```
what if…

you stopped.
just for a minute.

everything works again
after you unplug it.

even you.
```

**C — the minute reclaimed** (mirrors "gone in a blur" in reverse; ties into
the stats screen's CTA "bring my minute back"):

```
what if…

you took one minute back.

no phone. no plans. no doing.
one minute of nothing.

every day.

it adds up to a life.
```

## Craft notes (quote research, 2026-06-10)

- **Kierkegaard** — "The unhappy man is never present to himself" →
  "but never really here" stands on this; strongest line on screen 2.
- **Annie Dillard** — "How we spend our days is how we spend our lives" →
  the day→life escalation behind "days, months, years — gone in a blur."
- **Ferris Bueller** — "Life moves pretty fast… you could miss it" →
  power comes from concreteness and plain speech, not abstractions.
- **John Lennon** — "Life is what happens while you're busy making other
  plans" → the inversion: what you're busy with eats what you're busy for.
- **John Lubbock** — "to lie sometimes on the grass… watching the clouds
  float across the sky, is by no means a waste of time."
- **Regina Brett** — "To do nothing and have it count for something."
- **Lewis Carroll** — would give all wealth "to be once more a little child
  for one bright summer-day."
- **A.A. Milne (Pooh)** — "Doing nothing often leads to the very best of
  something."
- **Mary Oliver** — "idle and blessed", "your one wild and precious life".
- **Anne Lamott** — "Almost everything will work again if you unplug it for
  a few minutes, including you." — best-fitting quote for this app.
- **Pascal** — "All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit
  quietly in a room alone."
- **Lao Tzu** — "Nature does not hurry, yet everything is accomplished."
- Rules of thumb: great triads never repeat one thought three times — they
  raise the stakes three times; concrete object + a twist that turns back on
  the reader ("including you", "you could miss it").
